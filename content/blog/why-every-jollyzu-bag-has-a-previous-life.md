Every raincoat starts with excitement.

We buy it, we love it, we wear it. Then, one day, it gets pushed to the back of a wardrobe, donated, or simply forgotten.
For many clothing items, there comes a point when they are no longer wanted. That's where JollyZu begins.
My job is to find these unwanted raincoats, jackets, waterproof fabrics, and other reclaimed textiles, and give them a second life. I source materials through donations, second-hand shops, and online platforms, looking for fabrics that still have plenty of life left in them.
The idea of creating something beautiful from discarded materials has been with me for years. I finally had the opportunity to turn that passion into reality and build a business around it. My goal isn't to claim that I can save the world, but to make a small, meaningful contribution by reducing textile waste and keeping valuable materials out of landfill.
Many people assume that donated clothing simply finds a new home. While some of it does, a significant amount is exported overseas, where it can end up in enormous textile waste sites. Some discarded fabrics eventually find their way into waterways and oceans, creating environmental problems that affect communities and ecosystems around the world.
By rescuing materials that already exist, I can create products without relying on newly manufactured fabrics.
Every JollyZu bag carries a story. Before becoming a bag, it may have been a waterproof jacket worn on rainy walks, a colourful raincoat loved by a child, or an outdoor garment that had simply reached the end of its original purpose. Instead of becoming waste, it becomes something useful again.


## Nothing starts from zero

I do not buy new fabric for bag bodies. Everything is rescued: offcuts from alterations, pre-loved clothes, deadstock, and textiles that would otherwise head for landfill.

That constraint sounds limiting. In practice, it is where the design happens. I am not sketching a bag and then ordering material to match. I am looking at what I have — the weight, the colour, the way it frays — and asking what it wants to become.

## Why "previous life" is the point

Fast fashion treats fabric as disposable. Upcycling treats it as a resource with history.

When you carry a JollyZu piece, you are carrying:

- **Less waste** — one less metre of new cotton or polyester produced
- **A unique object** — no two rescued bundles cut the same way twice
- **Slow fashion** — made by one person in Edinburgh, not a production line

## What you'll notice on each piece

Some bags still show traces of their past: a faded pocket line, a contrast stitch from an old seam, a patch in a colour that only makes sense if you know where it came from.

That is intentional. I do not sand away every clue. The previous life is part of the beauty.

## Care means keeping the story going

These bags are built to be used hard and repaired if needed. The best outcome is not a bag that looks factory-new forever — it is a bag that earns a *next* life on your arm.

If you have textiles at home you cannot bear to bin, get in touch — I am always curious what is hiding in the back of wardrobes.
